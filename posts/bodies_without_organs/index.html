Legitimacy as a Body-Without-Organs and the Failures of American Foreign Policy in the Middle East
2024-04-10 00:28 label US   Middle East   Deleuze   body-without-organs   legitimacy   ISIS   Islam   Salafism   universality   international relations  

The primary unit of international relations is the state. The state forms with the monopolization of legitimate violence in an area. The concept of legitimacy is imagined here as the Deleuzian concept of a body-without-organs. The organs form the function of the use of legitimacy. The organization of organs upon the body, I call a monad, borrowing from functional programming.

The monad arises in a rhizomatic way from sites of mobilization, on legitimacy, such as may be created by power vacuums, a line of flight, and finds in it a potential for mobilization and subjects them to the monad. The subject in turn gives way to the monad the capability to mobilize. The resource, the mechanism of the expenditure of that resource, and the organization of the product of such expenditure are still in the abstract here. It is the function of the organ that sets the narrative of the body’s organization, that limits either the immediate material expansion of that body or its abstract potential expansion. There is a degree of gravity from material embodiment in that organ on sites of mobilization, by which more subjects on the body are drawn to it. Note that without the material embodiment of an organ on the body, it is free to give way to new organs, or perhaps none at all, as perhaps Deleuze and Guatarri might have imagined.

The modern state is an amalgam of interworking and self-supporting sites of mobilization, which it chooses to engage in and to abstract a certain shape outward to embody. It is interworking and self-supporting in the sense that it often pushes to allow for a great degree of coherence to the narrative capability and capacity of the body, which is how nationalism, as an organ functions. Subjectivity is borne out of these pre-existing bodies, individually and organizationally. It makes a great deal of sense then that states will engage in sites that afford them the most capability and capacity. Similarly, the states must also be careful to balance their material fulfillment on these sites and for the potential of subjects themselves to project a new abstraction of the body, a way in which it may be organized, from which a new subjectivity can arise materially.

On the body-without-organs, limitless capability gives way to limitless potential; and that may push an organ to behave in a way it would not in isolation. For example, the American state, through its claim to universality has derived an immense amount of legitimacy historically, and from its historical legacies still continue to hold the material gravitational pull. If there is to be strategic solvency in American material commitments and capacity, there must also be a solvency in these sites where legitimacy is conferred. Past American failures can be characterized by huge lines of flight, without an accompanying organ to organize the open spaces of legitimacy, at the same time using weak sites of mobilization on miniscule spaces on the body, such as that of women’s autonomy, allowing for a rhizomatic rise of monads on pre-existing space on the body and to look towards new subjectivities. In the case of ISIS, as a monadic group, it is a subjectivity formed out of pre-existing sites of mobilization in revolutionary Salafism, but more broadly, as a current against the projected universality of the Western subjectivity embodied in that of the nation-state on the body. It is a subjectivity formed more out of negation of an organ than an affirmation.

Universality is a space on the body too wide for any one set of organs to occupy, and in its negation of certain organs on the body, the entire subject comes under threat. The U.S. state in its belligerency may not lead to its own collapse on the body, as attacks on its organs might, but the international system, back to a rhizomatic form, if it does.
